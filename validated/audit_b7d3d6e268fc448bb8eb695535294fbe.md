### Title
Unchecked `contract_address` in `execute_meta_tx_v0` Allows Any Contract to Execute `__execute__` on Behalf of Arbitrary Accounts - (File: `execution/syscall_impls.cairo`)

---

### Summary

The `execute_meta_tx_v0` syscall in `syscall_impls.cairo` never verifies that the calling contract is authorized to act on behalf of the `request.contract_address` it supplies. Any deployed contract can invoke this syscall with an arbitrary victim `contract_address` and attacker-controlled `calldata`, causing the OS to call `__execute__` on the victim account with no signature validation at the OS level. This is the direct analog of the DODO `decodeData.payer` bug: a caller-supplied identity used for fund-moving operations is never checked against the actual transaction initiator.

---

### Finding Description

`execute_meta_tx_v0` is a syscall available to **any executing contract** (dispatched unconditionally in `execute_syscalls.cairo` line 343–344). Its implementation in `syscall_impls.cairo` lines 286–400:

1. Reads `contract_address` and `calldata` entirely from the attacker-controlled `request` struct.
2. Computes `meta_tx_hash` over `(contract_address, selector, calldata, chain_id)` — all attacker-supplied.
3. Constructs a brand-new `TxInfo` with `account_contract_address = contract_address` (the victim), `signature = request.signature` (attacker-supplied), `version = 0`, `nonce = 0`.
4. Calls `contract_call_helper` → `select_execute_entry_point_func` → `__execute__` on `contract_address` with `caller_address = ORIGIN_ADDRESS` (0).

**At no point** is there a check that:
- `contract_address == caller_execution_context.execution_info.contract_address`, or
- `contract_address == old_tx_info.account_contract_address`, or
- any other authorization binding the caller to the target. [1](#0-0) 

The `old_tx_info` from the outer transaction is accessed only to extract `chain_id` — the outer transaction's `account_contract_address` is never compared to `contract_address`: [2](#0-1) 

The OS also performs **no signature verification** itself. The signature is merely forwarded into the new `TxInfo`: [3](#0-2) 

The execution context passed to the victim's `__execute__` sets `caller_address = ORIGIN_ADDRESS`: [4](#0-3) 

The syscall is reachable by any contract — it is the final `else` branch in `execute_syscalls`, with no caller restriction: [5](#0-4) 

The `meta_tx_hash` computation (`compute_meta_tx_v0_hash`) uses the deprecated Pedersen-based v0 hash over attacker-controlled inputs: [6](#0-5) 

Additionally, the new `TxInfo` hardcodes `nonce = 0` and performs no nonce increment on the victim's state, meaning the same meta-transaction can be replayed indefinitely within a block or across blocks. [7](#0-6) 

---

### Impact Explanation

Standard StarkNet account contracts (OpenZeppelin and equivalents) place signature verification exclusively in `__validate__`. The `__execute__` entry point simply iterates over the provided call array and dispatches each call. Because `execute_meta_tx_v0` bypasses `__validate__` entirely and calls `__execute__` directly, an attacker can supply arbitrary `calldata` encoding token-transfer calls (e.g., ERC-20 `transfer` to the attacker's address) and have them executed under the victim account's identity. This constitutes **direct loss of funds** for any account that holds token balances.

**Impact: Critical — Direct loss of funds.**

---

### Likelihood Explanation

- The attacker only needs to deploy a contract (permissionless on StarkNet) and invoke the `meta_tx_v0` syscall from within it.
- No privileged role, leaked key, or social engineering is required.
- Any account that has ever approved a spender or holds a native token balance is at risk.
- The `nonce = 0` hardcoding means no replay protection exists at the OS level, amplifying the damage.

**Likelihood: High.**

---

### Recommendation

Add an authorization check at the start of `execute_meta_tx_v0` that binds the target `contract_address` to the calling contract's identity. The minimal fix is one of:

**Option A** — Require the caller to be acting on its own behalf:
```cairo
// In execute_meta_tx_v0, after reading contract_address:
assert contract_address = caller_execution_info.contract_address;
```

**Option B** — Require the target to match the outer transaction's account:
```cairo
assert contract_address = old_tx_info.account_contract_address;
```

Additionally, the OS should either:
- Verify the provided signature against `meta_tx_hash` before dispatching, or
- Increment the victim account's nonce to prevent replay.

---

### Proof of Concept

1. Attacker deploys `MaliciousContract` on StarkNet.
2. Attacker submits an invoke transaction calling `MaliciousContract.__execute__`.
3. Inside `MaliciousContract.__execute__`, the contract issues a `meta_tx_v0` syscall with:
   - `contract_address = <victim_account>`
   - `selector = EXECUTE_ENTRY_POINT_SELECTOR`
   - `calldata = [1, <erc20_address>, transfer_selector, 2, <attacker_address>, <amount>]` (a single-call execute payload)
   - `signature = []` (empty — never verified by the OS)
4. The OS computes `meta_tx_hash` over the attacker-supplied inputs, constructs a `TxInfo` with `account_contract_address = victim_account`, and calls `victim_account.__execute__` with the transfer calldata.
5. The victim account's `__execute__` (which does not re-verify the signature) dispatches the ERC-20 transfer, sending the victim's tokens to the attacker.
6. Because `nonce = 0` is hardcoded and never incremented, step 2–5 can be repeated in subsequent transactions until the victim's balance is drained.
