### Title
Recipient Not Bound by MPC Signature Allows Front-Running to Redirect Bridged Tokens — (`solana/programs/bridge_token_factory/src/instructions/user/finalize_transfer.rs`)

### Summary
The `recipient` account in `FinalizeTransfer` and `FinalizeTransferSol` is an `UncheckedAccount` with no constraint tying it to the MPC-signed payload. Because `FinalizeTransferPayload` contains no `recipient` field, the MPC signature does not commit to any Solana recipient pubkey. Any relayer (or front-runner) can substitute an arbitrary `recipient` while reusing a valid MPC signature, redirecting minted or unlocked tokens to an attacker-controlled account.

### Finding Description
`FinalizeTransferPayload` is the struct that the MPC signs. It contains `destination_nonce`, `transfer_id`, `amount`, and `fee_recipient` (a NEAR account string) — but **no Solana recipient pubkey**. [1](#0-0) 

The `recipient` account is declared as `UncheckedAccount` with only the comment `"this can be any type of account"` and no seeds, address, or payload-binding constraint. [2](#0-1) 

The `token_account` is derived as `associated_token::authority = recipient`, so tokens are minted/transferred to the ATA of whoever is passed as `recipient`. [3](#0-2) 

For the SOL variant, the transfer goes directly to `recipient.to_account_info()` with no binding check. [6](#0-5) 

### Impact Explanation
An attacker who observes a pending `finalize_transfer` transaction in the mempool (or who acts as the relayer) can copy the valid `SignedPayload<FinalizeTransferPayload>` and resubmit it with their own pubkey as `recipient`. The `used_nonces` check consumes the nonce, so the legitimate recipient's transaction will then fail. Tokens (SPL or SOL) land in the attacker's account. This is direct theft of bridged assets — Critical impact. [7](#0-6) 

### Likelihood Explanation
Any unprivileged actor who can submit Solana transactions can execute this. No special keys or roles are needed — only the ability to observe and front-run a `finalize_transfer` call, which is straightforward on Solana given priority fees. The relayer role itself is permissionless, so the attacker can simply be the relayer. [8](#0-7) 

### Recommendation
Include the Solana `recipient` pubkey inside `FinalizeTransferPayload` so it is covered by the MPC signature. Then add an Anchor constraint on the `recipient` account that verifies `recipient.key() == data.payload.recipient`. This ensures the MPC-attested recipient is the only valid destination. [1](#0-0) 

### Proof of Concept
1. A legitimate NEAR→Solana transfer is initiated for Alice (`alice_pubkey`).
2. The MPC produces a valid `SignedPayload<FinalizeTransferPayload>` (signing `destination_nonce`, `transfer_id`, `amount`, `fee_recipient` — no recipient pubkey).
3. Attacker observes the pending `finalize_transfer` transaction.
4. Attacker resubmits `finalize_transfer` with identical `SignedPayload` but passes `attacker_pubkey` as `recipient`.
5. Anchor derives `token_account` as the ATA of `attacker_pubkey`.
6. Signature verification passes (recipient not in signed data); nonce is consumed; tokens are minted/transferred to attacker's ATA.
7. Alice's subsequent `finalize_transfer` fails with nonce-already-used error. [9](#0-8)
